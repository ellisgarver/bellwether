what bellwether is built on

the inspiration, the literature and work which surrounds it, and what it was built for.

a tool for reading the recent past

bellwether serves as an educational and historical-analysis tool spanning more than fifteen years of macro-financial discourse: how stories like the 2013 taper tantrum, the transitory-inflation debate, and the 2023 soft-landing turn emerged, took hold, peaked, and faded, in the words of the institutions that shaped them, alongside how the most recent stories are unfolding both in discourse and press.

built to make an exciting yet abstract idea more concrete.

the narrative-economics idea

the starting point is Robert Shiller's Narrative Economics (2017 AEA presidential address; 2019 book) which details how economic stories spread through a population the way contagious ideas do: a story is picked up, carried for a while, and eventually dropped. the stories people inevitably carry shape the economic decisions they make, so what spreads matters as much as analyzing the fundamentals.

bellwether takes the measurable part of that idea, that narratives have life-cycles, and reads them directly from the record: each stage comes straight from the volume trajectory of institutional macro writing, model-free, so no reading depends on any one theory of how stories spread being right. the contagion picture above survives as one of three interpretive lenses fit alongside (epidemic, growth, and adoption curves), each a different account of the same measured shape, shown with how well it actually fits that story.

field-standard parameters

every parameter in the pipeline is a published library default or a value taken from the primary literature with a citation. all of them live in a single configuration file, and every random step flows from one fixed seed ("Don't Panic."). where no field-accepted anchor exists, the parameter has been removed, and parameters have not been adjusted to make the output look better.

see the full methodology, with citations in METHODOLOGY.md from the repository.

references & related work

similarly, the pipeline itself stands on named and field respected research at every step: Shiller's narrative-economics program, the text-as-data literature in economics, the growth, epidemic, and diffusion models behind the lenses, and the embed/cluster and trend-test methods — ranging from Verhulst (1838) to the current LLM-frontier papers.

see the full references and the surrounding literature, paper by paper, in related_work.md from the repository.
