how bellwether works

the pipeline that turns institutional writing into clustered narratives and lifecycle analysis.

from articles to narratives

each article is split into passages of roughly 512 tokens, the retrieval unit from the BEIR benchmark, so long reports are read in coherent pieces. each passage becomes a vector from a sentence-embedding model (Qwen3-Embedding-8B, 4,096 numbers per passage) in a space where closeness tracks meaning: two passages making similar arguments land near each other numerically.

those vectors are clustered with BERTopic, which reduces them to a lower dimension (UMAP), finds dense regions of related passages (HDBSCAN), and then names each region by the terms that are frequent within but rare elsewhere (a class-based TF-IDF). density-based clustering leaves passages belonging to no clear group as outliers, and each surviving group is a narrative candidate.

scope is decided after clustering with each cluster matched to the standard JEL economics taxonomy published by the American Economic Association. this is done by comparing the cluster's center to a prototype vector for each JEL field and taking the nearest one. clusters in the macro-finance fields, JEL E (macroeconomics and monetary), F (international), G (financial), and H (public and fiscal), are the core scope; clusters that fall outside those fields are still fit and shown, just flagged with the external JEL field they landed in.

the display names come from a small language model (llama 3.1-8B) . it reads each cluster's terms, excerpts from its most central articles, its date span, and its source mix, then writes a short title and description. a defined cluster here is a long-lived family of related writing rather than a single news story. new events usually land inside an old family (ex. the 2026 Iran war joins sanctions clusters from 2010).

four lenses, a stage, and statistical analysis

counting a narrative's articles per week gives a volume curve: a time series of how loud the story is. that count is then normalized for how much the whole corpus published that day, so a story doesn't look like it's growing just because coverage overall grew. four lenses are applied to the adjusted curve and shown side by side. each lens encodes a different assumption about how a story spreads:

logistic growth (Verhulst, 1838)

a characteristic S-shaped rise to a saturation ceiling, governed by a growth rate and a carrying capacity.

SIR epidemic (Kermack & McKendrick, 1927)

treats attention as an outbreak that spreads, peaks, and burns out, measured by doubling and halving speed.

Bass diffusion (Bass, 1969)

shows adoption via innovators (who initiate the story) and imitators (who pick it up from others), with a coefficient for each.

shape facts

model-free readings taken straight from a view of the true curve: time to peak, total volume, number of distinct waves.

each curve is fit by bounded least squares and scored with R², the share of the volume's variation the curve explains. because many narratives have multiple peaks or long quiet periods where no lifecycle model applies, each lens searches for the sub-span of at least one-third of the narrative's timeline where its fit is strongest — so the R² reflects a real episode, not a diluted average across inactive stretches. every lens stays available on every narrative, opening on the best-scoring one. weaker lenses are grayed not hidden, and the score sits on the tab. when no lens describes the narrative well it opens on the volume alone, and the grayed tabs still pull any lens up for comparison.

the lenses describe shape but don't assign stage. instead, the stage is read directly from the recent curve with a known model-free trend test: a modified Mann–Kendall test (Mann, 1945; Kendall, 1948) over the most recent four weeks, its variance corrected for the autocorrelation that smoothing introduces (Hamed & Rao, 1998). see more on the four narrative stages below:

  • growtha significant upward trend over the trailing four weeks (Mann–Kendall p < 0.05): attention is rising
  • stableno significant trend, and recent volume holds at 25% or more of the narrative's own peak level: a high plateau
  • decaya significant downward trend over the trailing four weeks (p < 0.05): attention is falling
  • dormantno significant trend, and recent volume has fallen below 25% of the narrative's own peak level: faded out

the last piece of analysis connects each narrative to the world outside the corpus: two bidirectional Granger tests (Granger, 1969), one against broad-press story counts (Media Cloud) and one against a market series (VIX), both computed on the unscaled weekly series. sweeping by lags of one to four weeks in both directions, the tests determine whether past values of one series help predict the other, and reports the strongest lag: whether the discourse moved before or after the press, and before or after the market, and by how much. note that Granger precedence measures timing and not cause. discourse can move first yet not impact press or markets.

reading a narrative

take US-China strategic economic dialogue for example. top to bottom, its page holds:

01 the bars +

weekly volume, adjusted for how much the whole corpus published that week — the measured story itself.

02 the lens tabs +

one per lens, each carrying its R². the chart opens on the best-fitting lens; grayed tabs are weak fits that stay clickable for comparison, and clicking the active tab clears the curve back to the bars alone.

03 pan & zoom +

drag pans the view; the two sliders zoom time and volume independently.

04 the stage readout +

the numbers behind the stage call: the trend direction, its p-value against α = 0.05, the recent log-slope, and recent volume as a share of the narrative's own peak.

05 the shape facts +

total and peak volume, time to peak, active days, and wave count, read straight off the curve.

06 the overlay checkboxes +

broad-press story counts (Media Cloud, thin before ~2017) and a market series (VIX), each min-max rescaled onto the volume axis — its own low at the floor, its own high at the tallest bar — so an overlay carries timing and shape, never height.

07 the lead-lag readout +

the two Granger verdicts, each direction reported with its significance, p-value, and best lag.

further down sits a reading list, three stacked lists linking to the source documents: the narrative's core pieces, its earliest writing, and its most recent. related narratives close the page, ranked three ways: by meaning (closeness of cluster centers in embedding space), by terms (shared distinctive vocabulary), and by curve shape (correlation of the two volume curves). close neighbors score high on all three.
